A real teacher never presses with knowledge. Not because there is something to hide — quite the opposite. It is simply that they long ago understood: the louder you sound, the less substance there is in you.

A student's timidity is understandable. It does not come from stupidity — it comes from intuition: you can feel the difference in the scale of experience. The teacher knows more, has seen more broadly, has drawn deeper conclusions. But that does not make them superior. Knowledge is not a currency you spend in exchange for respect.

Real respect does not grow from a demonstration of superiority. It grows from inner tact — the ability to be beside someone, not above them.

And here is what matters: being human is not measured by erudition. There are many intelligent people. There are fewer truly humane ones. And even if you and someone else are not on the same shelf in the library, that is no reason to slam books on the table.

There is a typical mistake beginners make: they try to impress with intellect. They start throwing around quotes, playing the seasoned analyst. And they don't notice how they have turned from students into contenders. A contender is not a student. They are no longer listening — they are performing. Performing the expert. It is a role, I assure you, that quickly bores anyone who has known the real thing.

An experienced person reads a great deal between the lines. They don't rush to conclusions, but they don't play the fool either. They value simplicity, because they know what it costs. So it is better to be natural. Neither fawning nor bristling. Respect without strain — that is the register in which you can actually hear the music of another person.
