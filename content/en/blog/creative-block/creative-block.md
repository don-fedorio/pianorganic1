Too much is undefined. The deadlines have long passed. Maybe I will not be able to write anything at all.

Well — *will die trying*.

---

Let me lay out the structure of the article one more time, and work out the problems as I go.

I will start with an introduction on the sonata in the USA as a genre. What even is "the state of the field"? I will try to picture people like myself who want to generate something in the academic space on my topic. There should be a limited number of them. Let there be three, besides me.

Each of them has made a contribution in the form of articles, books, or dissertations. I can construct my opening sentence like this:

*"A surge of interest in this question occurred in the 1960s... Researchers have examined the sonata genre in the context of its development in the United States, yet to date no attempt has been made to trace the stages of the sonata's formation as a phenomenon from its origins to the present."*

Relevance? Please, not "because of the current state of Russian-American relations." Let us leave it as:

*"The enduring relevance of this study lies in the opening of new knowledge about the formation of the American sonata from the perspective of Russian musicology."*

Fine. So what?

I am stuck at the very beginning. Alexander Reinagle wrote his Four Philadelphia Sonatas at the end of the eighteenth century. MacDowell wrote his four programmatic canvases at the end of the nineteenth.

What are we looking for in this article? Something new. And new means the imprint of American identity, the answer to the question "who are we in art?" With Reinagle, that is hard to find. With MacDowell, even a hundred years later, it is still impossible to break free from European tradition.

What is European in MacDowell's sonatas? Our subject came from the tradition of late Romanticism, under Liszt's influence. The parallels with Liszt are easy to find. So what? That is not news.

Where on earth is the novelty?
