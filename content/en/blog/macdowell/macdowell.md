Starting again after a break is hard. The brain is busy keeping the body alive — circulating blood, digesting food, clearing waste — and "extra" work is the last thing it wants. Any effort after a long rest genuinely feels like self-harm.

The brain has to accept that self-harm because its higher centres understand something simple: if the work is not done, nothing good lies ahead. So for a working mind, motivation is the motivation of heroic self-immolation for something great. And the anticipation of greatness has a way of persuading the lazy that the fight is worth it.

---

American composers of the period still looked to Europe for serious training. The key figure for professional music in the United States was Edward MacDowell, who studied at conservatories in France and Germany. It was in Germany that MacDowell had the opportunity to meet Franz Liszt — and the older composer's creative personality could not fail to leave its mark on the young American.

The late Liszt was a man who had survived great hardship and extraordinary heights, who genuinely sought to withdraw from worldwide fame as a performer and had chosen the path of the artist-priest. His contradictory work joins transcendent virtuosity with deep introspection. And Liszt's mentorship toward the younger generation of musicians who came to him in Weimar was no small thing — the Hungarian composer became a genuine spiritual authority for many of his students.

MacDowell became that same figure for the first generation of professional musicians in the United States — the founder of a professional school of composition there. In 1891, the first university music department in America was established at Columbia University, with MacDowell at its head. His work is a vivid example of the Romantic era.

Pedagogy is not only the technique of making and performing. It is also a moral compass pointing toward the pole of an epoch. Musical works cultivate taste, enrich the spirit, shape ideals. Such a view of pedagogy is, of course, not without its Romanticism — but what matters most is the inheritance of European thinking.

The lyrical figure of the Romantic artist will inevitably be charged with a wide spectrum of psychological contradictions: good and evil, the elevated and the earthly, the bodily and the spiritual, the natural and the social. All of this became the obligatory stock of the musical poetics of that time. American culture "dived" into Romanticism through MacDowell's work and activity. In his aesthetic sphere: nature, folklore, and the lyrical imagery of heroic deed and tragedy.
