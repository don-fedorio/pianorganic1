What was the barline invented for?

Rhythm is part of music's nature. Without rhythm, music loses its structure — it dissolves like a substance losing its form, becoming noise, losing all meaning.

If we call any sonic phenomenon music, then rhythm must be present in it. You can manage without harmony. You can manage without melody. Rhythm is the irreducible property of music — any music, even the most radical avant-garde.

To proclaim the absence of rhythm in some hypothetical ultra-avant-garde music is like speaking of the absence of H₂O in water. The very phenomenon of music is destroyed the moment rhythm is extracted from it.

At the root of rhythm lies the flow of time. By measuring it in equal portions, we obtain temporal periods — isolated segments that memory is capable of grasping. Barlines separate one segment from another, one working unit from the next.

One of the barline's functions is the visual representation of these temporal segments. The pulse of measures lays the foundation for the unfolding of a musical idea in a particular direction — all the way down to genre and style.

To work with measures is to command the musical fabric — to find the hidden patterns and connections within it.
