Memory is capricious matter — musical memory especially. Anyone who has ever performed from memory knows: it is not simple repetition of something learned, not a mechanical routine, but something far more delicate and alive.

The Scottish pianist Lilia MacKinnon wrote about this with remarkable sensitivity in her book *Music by Heart* — one of the few works devoted specifically to memorised performance. She introduces a beautiful image: the "book of experience" — an inner chronicle in which we record our personal discoveries and sensations. Every touch of a sound, every act of performance, is a new page.

Sound cannot be "fixed." It is born and dies — and in that lies its beauty. Memory does not frame music. Rather, it makes possible something else: living through the music again, each time. Even what has long been "learned" demands attention, adaptation, immersion in the particular moment. Because sounding is always here and now.

Playing from memory is not about rote learning. It is a practice of live response, attentiveness, and freedom. And if anything, it is not memory that holds the music — it may be precisely that music preserves our memory, as a connecting thread between who we were, who we are, and who we are still becoming.
