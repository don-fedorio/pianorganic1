*Identical* is a two-voice piano piece with a short middle episode, where several voices accumulate before the original texture returns. Key of A minor, time signature 5/4, tempo Andante.

The composer offers no programmatic notes explaining the title, yet the instructive logic of the work is easy to detect — it lies in the connection between the title and the scrupulous care with which the fingering is marked. After a brief look, one notices that the fingering in the left and right hands is identical, creating what might be called a "finger canon" or "finger imitation." The rhythmic pattern of the motifs repeats almost everywhere, making the idea of *sameness* easy to read.

The opening modulating period sustains a strict rhythmic canon, and by the end of the phrase *identicalness* has become something familiar. In the middle episode the repetition of motifs grows less obvious — layered structures build in both hands simultaneously. The "lagging sameness" of the fingers persists, making the fingering choices look somewhat unusual, but they are meant to be followed without exception.

## Multi-Level Sameness

Sameness as a category can manifest not only sequentially in time but simultaneously. Both techniques are present in the piece. At first the fingering patterns in both hands echo each other by entering in turn; then toward the end of the phrase the left hand "catches up" with the right, and both voices sound in rhythmic unison with mirrored fingering.

Mirror reflection is an example of sameness. But if the reflected object is not identical to its mirror image, that relationship is called *chirality*.

A chiral object becomes so because of the particular complexity of its structure. Spheres, cubes, cylinders and other symmetrical shapes are not chiral — you can multiply them in a mirror endlessly and they remain geometrically identical. By contrast, the fifth finger of the left hand is not identical to the fifth finger of the right: these two objects are chiral with respect to each other. That is the situation with the fingering here.

The principle of chirality is present at every level. The choice of the asymmetric time signature 5/4 (symbolically linked to five fingers), 20 measures arranged in five rows of four, and a clearly defined formal centre functioning as a mirror plane — with two periods arranged symmetrically on either side — all of this points to a single underlying pattern governing the entire work.

## The Mirror Portal in the Texture

If the middle section of *Identical* functions as a mirror surface, then the thickening of the texture approaching the recapitulation makes perfect sense. Beyond the obvious harmonic preparation, there is an artistic analogy here: the approach through the mirror into the looking-glass world. A mirror is a portal into the opposite dimension. The closer you approach your own reflection, the larger it appears, until it fills the entire field of view. This is why the texture grows so dense at the middle of the piece — almost to the point of clustering.

Functionally, this borderline proximity of two worlds is expressed in rising tension, the introduction of chromatic sounds and "creeping" intonations — a strong contrast to the more open texture of the outer sections.

## Chirality as an Expressive Factor

A break in symmetry is always a departure from equilibrium — one that sets a process of active stabilisation in motion, a search for resolution. That is how harmonic functionality works; that is how musical form develops.

The 5/4 time signature already introduces instability. Musical phrases inside the periods seem not to fit neatly into the metric grid, and this itself breaks the stasis. The opening period is modulating — stepping outside the home key is an entry into the "mirror portal": a dynamic transitional space between two relatively calm outer sections.

And finally, the work turns out to be semantically dense:
- the role of the number 5 (five fingers, five beats, five rows);
- two outer periods divided by the middle as a mirror surface;
- imitation as the vehicle of sameness;
- different melodic shapes performed with obligatory chiral fingering.

The instruction to play with identical fingers — which at first glance looks purely technical — proves on closer examination to be a powerful expressive tool. The artistic whole in performance is embodied through the fingering itself, whose logic turns out to be directly connected to the deep structure of the musical text.
